This is your computational science portfolio. It's built around one idea: your thinking comes first. The computer helps you test your ideas — but the ideas are always yours. This guide walks you through how to use every part of the site.
THE BIG IDEA Your thinking is the product

Every experiment follows the same pattern. You predict what will happen, then you test it with a simulation, then you explain what you found. An AI Coach then asks you one question to push your thinking further.

The simulation is just a tool. Your hypothesis and your interpretation are the real work.

step 01 Write your hypothesis

Before you touch anything, write down what you think will happen. Be specific. For example: "I predict that shots from the centre of the penalty box will have the highest probability because the angle to the goal is widest."

When you're happy with your prediction, click "Lock hypothesis and begin". This timestamps your prediction and saves it — you can't change it after locking. That's the point: it proves this was your thinking before seeing the data.

Why lock it? In real science, you commit to your prediction before running the experiment. It keeps you honest and makes your results more meaningful.
step 02 Run the experiment

Once your hypothesis is locked, the lab opens up. Each experiment has different controls — sliders, buttons, or a canvas where you click to set things up.

Play around. Move the sliders. Place shots on the pitch. Add planets. Change the temperature. The simulation updates in real time so you can see what happens.

When you're ready, click the button to run the full simulation (like "Run 500 simulations" in the xG experiment).

step 03 See the results

After the simulation runs, you'll see charts, numbers, and summaries. These show you what actually happened — the data. Compare this to what you predicted in Step 1.

Did it match your hypothesis? Were you surprised? What pattern do you notice?

step 04 Talk to your AI Coach

Write what you think the results mean in the interpretation box. Be honest — if something surprised you, say so. If you're not sure about something, say that too.

Click "Ask coach" and your AI Coach will read your hypothesis, your data, and your interpretation. It will ask you exactly one question — never gives you the answer, just pushes your thinking.

You can reply, and the Coach will ask another question. This back-and-forth is saved automatically.

The Coach's rules: It never tells you the answer. It never explains the science to you. It just asks sharp questions that make you think harder. That's what good mentors do.
NAVIGATE What's on the home page

Your portfolio home page has three main sections:

Experiments (7 modules) — These are the core of your portfolio. They follow the 4-step flow above. Start with Experiment 01 and work through them in order. Each one teaches you something different: probability, gravity, chaos, fractals, random processes, and astrophysics.

Computational Tools (7 research tools) — These are more advanced simulations for deeper exploration. They include Kinetic Monte Carlo, Quantum Monte Carlo, the Ising Model, Percolation, Diffusion-Limited Aggregation, Lattice Boltzmann fluids, and Molecular Dynamics. Use these once you've finished the core experiments.

Engineering (5 modules) — The Arduino section. Simulate DC motors, servos, and PID controllers. If you plug in a real Arduino, you can control actual hardware from your browser.

EXPERIMENTS Quick reference
01
Where Do Goals Come From?
Place football shots on a pitch. The computer calculates the probability of each shot going in (xG). Run 500 simulations to see how many goals you'd score on average.
02
Planets That Never Collide
Build a mini solar system. Drag to set speed and direction. Find the sweet spot between flying away and crashing in. Discover what makes orbits stable.
03
The Edge of Chaos
One simple equation: multiply, subtract, repeat. Drag a slider and watch order suddenly break into chaos. Deterministic but unpredictable.
04
How Long Is a Coastline?
Measure a fractal with smaller and smaller rulers. The length keeps growing toward infinity, but the area stays finite. Build Koch snowflakes to see why.
05
The Drunkard's Walk
Send hundreds of random walkers stumbling around. After N steps, they spread out by only √N. Four times more steps = only twice as far.
06
Two Pendulums, Two Futures
Start two double pendulums with almost the same angle (difference of 0.001°). Watch them diverge completely. Chaos in action.
07
Reading Starlight
Plot real stars by temperature vs brightness. Structure appears: the Main Sequence, Red Giants, White Dwarfs. Data telling a physical story.
LOGBOOK Your research journal

Everything you do is saved automatically in your browser. Your hypotheses, timestamps, simulation results, and coach dialogues are all stored locally.

The logbook page pulls all your entries together in one place. You can search, filter by experiment, and export your notes.

Important: Your data is saved in this browser on this device. If you clear your browser data or switch to a different computer, your progress resets. That's fine — your work is about the thinking, not the saved state.
TIPS Good to know
1. Work in order. Experiments 01 through 07 build on each other. Start with the xG model and go from there.
2. Write in your own words. Don't try to sound fancy. Say what you actually think. That's what makes your portfolio real.
3. It's OK to be wrong. A hypothesis that doesn't match the data is not a failure — it's interesting. Write about why you were surprised.
4. Refresh to reset. If something looks stuck, refresh the page. Your locked hypothesis and saved data will still be there.
5. Works offline. After the page loads the first time, everything runs in your browser. No internet needed (except for the AI Coach).
6. Mobile works too. You can run experiments on your phone, but a laptop or desktop is better for the simulations with lots of controls.
ARDUINO Connecting real hardware

The Arduino section can connect to a real Arduino board plugged into your computer via USB. This means you can control actual motors and servos from the same web page that runs the simulation.

What works right now:

● Read live RPM data from a DC motor encoder and overlay it on the simulation chart
● Send pulse widths directly to a servo motor connected to the Arduino
● Push PID tuning gains (Kp, Ki, Kd) to the Arduino and compare simulation vs real hardware response

What you need:

Browser
Chrome or Edge (Web Serial API is not supported in Firefox or Safari)
Connection
HTTPS required (GitHub Pages works perfectly)
Hardware
Arduino Uno/Nano + USB cable + motor or servo
Arduino sketch
Flash a sketch that reads serial commands and sends sensor data back

Useful open-source Arduino libraries (GitHub):

arduino/Arduino The Arduino IDE and core libraries — start here
arduino-libraries/Servo Official servo motor control library
br3ttb/Arduino-PID-Library PID controller — matches the PID tuner in the portal
firmata/arduino Firmata protocol — lets your browser control Arduino pins directly
PowerBroker2/SerialTransfer Structured serial communication (packets instead of raw text)
How it works: Click "Connect Arduino" in the serial bar at the top of the Arduino portal. Your browser will ask you to select the USB port. Once connected, the green dot lights up, and live data starts flowing. The Arduino sketch on your board needs to match the serial protocol expected by the portal (baud rate, message format).
