step 02 Method — Understanding random walks What is a random walk and why does it matter?

A random walk is the simplest model of unpredictable motion. At each step, the walker moves in a random direction. Despite this total randomness, a precise mathematical law emerges from the chaos.

1D walk: The walker steps left or right with equal probability. After N steps, their position is the sum of N random +1/-1 choices.

2D walk: The walker steps in a random direction (any angle 0–360°). Their position after N steps is a vector sum of N random directions.

The key prediction: After N steps, the average distance from the origin is proportional to √N — not N. This means 4× more steps gives only 2× the spread. 100× more steps gives only 10× the spread. This is the square-root law.
Step distributions: With uniform steps, every step is the same size. With Gaussian steps, step sizes vary — most are small but some are large. Does the √N law still hold?

Measuring spread: We use RMS distance (root-mean-square) — square every walker's distance, average, then take the square root. RMS is more mathematically natural than simple average distance.
GUIDE Understanding the Numbers What the statistics mean and what to look for
RMS Distance — “How far did they spread?”

After N steps, each walker ends up somewhere. Some are close to the origin, some are far. The RMS (root-mean-square) distance is a single number that captures the typical spread. It’s calculated by squaring every walker’s distance, averaging, then taking the square root.

Why not just use the average distance? RMS gives more weight to walkers that ventured far, and it’s the natural quantity that the √N law predicts.

The √N Law — The Key Discovery

After N random steps of size s, the RMS distance from the origin is approximately s × √N. This means:

  • 100 steps → spread of about 10 × step size
  • 400 steps (4× more) → spread of about 20 (only 2× more, since √4 = 2)
  • 10,000 steps → spread of about 100 (not 10,000!)

The ratio (actual / theory) in the analysis section tests this. If it’s close to 1.0, the law holds. The √N law test checks it differently: it divides RMS at full steps by RMS at quarter steps — should be ≈ 2.0.

The RMS Chart — How To Read It

The solid line is the actual RMS distance at each step. The dashed line is the theoretical √N prediction. If they overlap, the theory is confirmed. Watch how the actual line fluctuates around the theoretical one — this is randomness in action.

With more walkers, the actual line gets smoother (closer to theory). With fewer walkers, it’s noisier. This is the power of large samples.

The Histogram — Where Do Walkers End Up?

In 1D, the histogram shows final positions along the number line. It should look like a bell curve centred at zero — most walkers stay near the origin, few wander very far.

In 2D, it shows the distribution of distances from the origin (always positive). The peak is not at zero because in 2D, there are more directions to wander away than back.

step 05 Context — Where random walks appear The same √N law shows up everywhere

The random walk isn't just a mathematical curiosity. The same √t scaling law appears in physics, biology, finance, and computer science. Every time something moves unpredictably, the random walk is there.

Brownian Motion

In 1827, botanist Robert Brown watched pollen grains jittering in water under a microscope. In 1905, Einstein proved they were being kicked by invisible water molecules — and that the average displacement grows as √t. This was one of the first proofs that atoms exist.

Einstein, 1905

Stock Prices

The "random walk hypothesis" says stock prices change unpredictably — each day's change is like a random step. If true, the spread of possible future prices grows as √t, not t. This is why long-term investing is hard to predict but short-term swings are even more random.

Bachelier, 1900

Diffusion

Drop ink into water. It spreads. The radius of the ink cloud grows as √t — exactly the random walk law. Each ink molecule is being kicked randomly by water molecules, doing a 3D random walk. Heat spreads the same way.

Fick's Law, 1855

DNA Polymers

A long polymer chain (like DNA) is a 3D random walk of molecular segments. The end-to-end distance of a chain with N segments is proportional to √N. This explains why DNA packs so efficiently — it's not stretched out, it's a random coil.

Flory, 1953
