Research Statement

I want to understand how complex behaviour emerges from simple rules. A football match produces thousands of shots across a season, but a geometric model with just two variables — distance and angle — can predict scoring probability. A surface made of silicon and germanium atoms follows basic energy rules, yet produces intricate pit patterns when etched. A random walker takes completely unpredictable steps, but after enough of them, a precise mathematical law appears.

My approach is always the same: start with a question, build a model, run the computation, and see if the prediction matches reality. The code is a tool. The thinking is the product.

Every experiment in this portfolio begins with a hypothesis I wrote before seeing any results. The simulations amplify my thinking — they do not replace it.

My Method

1
Ask a question. What controls surface roughness? Does stickiness change fractal dimension? How far does a random walker get?
2
Write a prediction. Before running anything, I write down what I expect to happen and why. This is locked with a timestamp — I cannot change it after the fact.
3
Build and run. I use computational models that implement real physics: Kinetic Monte Carlo, Tersoff potentials, diffusion-limited aggregation, Monte Carlo sampling.
4
Interpret the results. I compare what happened against what I predicted. Where was I right? Where was I wrong? What did I miss?
5
Go deeper. An AI coach asks me one Socratic question at a time — never gives me the answer. I have to reason my way through.

What Drives Me

I play football competitively, which is where my first computational question came from: Can you predict where goals will come from using just geometry? That question led to the xG model, which led to Monte Carlo simulation, which led to random walks, which led to materials science. Each answer opens a new question.

I am not interested in memorising formulas. I want to build things that work and understand why they work. Every portal in this portfolio is a tool I built to answer a question I actually cared about.
