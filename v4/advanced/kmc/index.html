SiGe Thin Film Simulator
This wraps reference Fortran 90 KMC code for Silicon-Germanium thin-film etching (kept in FortranReference/KMC/). I ported the physics faithfully to run in the browser via a Web Worker, and built visualisations, sweeps, and roughness-analysis dashboards around it. The core physics — desorption, surface diffusion, bulk diffusion with Metropolis criterion — came from the research implementation; what I added was instrumentation and exploration tooling.
Honest framing: This is learning work — I did not originate the KMC algorithm. I followed published research code, ported it to JavaScript, and wrapped it in a UI so I could experiment with parameters and see the results. The portal here is my instrumentation; the physics is not mine.
GUIDE Understanding the Numbers ▼ collapse
The big picture: You are simulating what happens when a Silicon-Germanium surface gets etched — atoms are stripped away, leaving behind a landscape of bumps and pits. The numbers below help you describe what that landscape looks like and how it got that way.
RMS Roughness — “How bumpy is the surface?”

RMS roughness is the standard deviation of surface heights — a single number capturing how far, on average, each point sits from the mean height.

Think of it like: Running your hand across a table. Small RMS = polished smooth. Large RMS = gravel road.

What to look for: Watch RMS grow over time. Does it plateau (saturate) or keep climbing?

Formula: RMS = √(⟨[h(x) − ⟨h⟩]²⟩)

β (Beta) — “How fast does roughness grow?”

Beta is the slope on a log-log plot of RMS vs iteration — a power law: RMS ∝ tβ. It tells you how fast the surface gets rougher.

Think of it like: The steepness of a hill. Steeper slope = surface roughens faster.

What to look for: Use the fit range buttons (Early / Growth / Full) to fit β over different regions. A straight line on the log-log chart means a clean power law. Record β along with T, θ, and P_des.

  • β ≈ 0.25 — Edwards-Wilkinson (slow, smooth)
  • β ≈ 0.33 — KPZ (moderate)
  • β ≈ 0.5 — Random deposition (fast, noisy)
α (Alpha) & ξ (Xi) — “How does roughness vary across distance?”

α (roughness exponent) tells you how height differences grow with separation distance. ξ (correlation length) is the distance beyond which heights are unrelated — the typical feature size.

Think of it like: ξ is the size of the “bubbles” on the surface. If ξ = 50, bumps and pits are about 50 sites wide. α tells you how jagged each bump is.

What to look for: On the G(r) chart, the rising slope gives α and the flat region starts at ξ.

Skewness & Kurtosis — “What shape is the height distribution?”

Skewness measures lopsidedness: negative = deep pits (long low tail), positive = tall peaks. Kurtosis measures extremes: high kurtosis = unusually deep pits or tall spikes.

Think of it like: Skewness = is the surface dented down or pushed up? Kurtosis = are there extreme outliers?

What to look for: Negative skewness + high kurtosis = scattered deep pits — the signature of preferential Ge etching. Enable the Skew/Kurt toggle to track these during a run.

Pit Analysis — “Where are the holes, and how big are they?”

Identifies depressions in the surface — regions significantly below the mean. Reports count, width, depth, and coverage.

Think of it like: Counting craters on the moon and measuring each one. More craters = more Ge was etched away.

What to look for: Pit count and coverage change with θ and temperature. Do pits get wider or deeper?

Etch Depth & Selectivity — “How much material was removed?”

Etch depth = how far the average surface has moved down. Selectivity = Ge atoms removed per Si atom removed. High selectivity means the etchant strongly prefers Ge.

Think of it like: Etch depth is how deep the hole gets. Selectivity is how picky the etchant is about what it removes.

What to look for: Higher P_des increases etch depth. High selectivity creates pits at Ge-rich sites.

Suggested Single-Run Experiments

Start with the Standard preset and run a full simulation. Watch the lattice evolve and the charts update. Then try changing one parameter at a time:

  1. Temperature: Run at 300K, 500K, 800K, 1000K. Record β, pit count, RMS roughness. Does higher temperature make the surface smoother or rougher? Why?
  2. Ge fraction (θ): Try 0.05, 0.2, 0.5. With more germanium, do you get more pits? Bigger pits? Does the skewness change?
  3. Desorption probability (P_des): Try 0.01, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5. Higher P_des means atoms leave the surface more easily. What happens to etch depth and roughness?
  4. Lattice size (lattx): Try 128, 512, 1024 with the same conditions. Does β change? This tests whether your result depends on system size or is a true physical property.
What To Record in Your Logbook
After each run, open the logbook and note:
  1. Parameters you used (T, θ, P_des, lattx, niter1)
  2. Final β value and the fit range you used
  3. Final RMS roughness
  4. Pit count, average pit width, and pit coverage
  5. α and ξ from the correlation chart
  6. Skewness and kurtosis — are they positive or negative? What does that tell you?
  7. What the lattice cross-section looks like — is it smooth, pitted, or very rough?
  8. Anything that surprised you or contradicted your prediction
