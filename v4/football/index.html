Where Do Goals Come From?
A computational investigation into Expected Goals (xG) — the probability model that professional clubs use to evaluate every shot. You will build two mathematical models, test them with Monte Carlo simulation, validate against real league data, and investigate what the numbers say about scoring that intuition alone cannot reveal.

step 02 Method — Understanding xG How do we assign probability to a shot?

xG assigns a probability (0 to 1) to every shot based on where and how it was taken. Two models, two approaches to the same question — compare them.

Geometric model: Calculates the angle subtended at the goal posts from the shot position, then applies exponential distance decay. Pure geometry — wider angle and shorter distance both increase xG.

Logistic regression model: Uses coefficients fitted to thousands of real shots. The equation weights angle, distance, and their interaction differently based on what actually happened in real matches.

WHAT MODIFIES xG BEYOND POSITION?
Shot type: Open play (baseline), headers (×0.6 — harder to direct), free kicks (×0.5 — wall and distance), penalties (fixed 0.76).

Situation: Defensive pressure (low/medium/high), assist type (through ball +15%, cut-back +20%, cross −10%), counter-attacks (+10%), 1v1 with keeper (+30%).

These modifiers capture what geometry alone misses: the context of the shot.
