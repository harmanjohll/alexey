Fractal dimension & DLA

Shapes that live between the integers.

The question. What is the length of Britain's coastline? If you measure with a 100 km ruler, you get one answer. A 10 km ruler — longer. A 1 km ruler — longer still. The measured length depends on the ruler. So: how long is it really?

The rule

For a fractal shape, mass within radius r grows as a power law:
M(r) ∝ rD,
where D is the fractal dimension.

A smooth line → D = 1.
A filled square → D = 2.
A fractal sits between: jagged enough to fill more space than a line, too sparse to fill a plane.

Real fractals have non-integer dimensions. A coastline typically sits around D ≈ 1.2–1.3. A diffusion-limited aggregate (DLA) cluster comes in at D ≈ 1.71 in 2D. Lightning bolts, river networks, blood vessels, bronchial trees — all fractals, all with their own characteristic D.

Why it matters

Fractal dimension is the rigorous answer to "how jagged is this shape?" It's the single number that captures how a structure fills space. In biology it correlates with how efficiently lungs and blood vessels distribute oxygen. In materials science it controls surface roughness and thin-film growth. In geography it quantifies terrain. One number, huge reach.

Worked example · Koch snowflake

Start with an equilateral triangle. Replace the middle third of each side with two sides of a smaller triangle. Repeat.

Each iteration multiplies the perimeter by 4/3. After n iterations: perimeter = (4/3)n × original. This diverges to infinity — the snowflake's perimeter grows without bound — yet the enclosed area stays finite. An infinitely long curve wrapped around a finite region.

Its fractal dimension: D = log 4 / log 3 ≈ 1.2619. More than a line, less than a plane.

Before you run the simulation

  1. Why does a coastline's measured length depend on the length of your ruler?
  2. What do you expect the fractal dimension of a DLA cluster to be — 1, 2, or somewhere between? Sketch one and compare.
  3. If a shape has dimension 1.7, what does that tell you about how densely it fills space — closer to a line or closer to a square?

Common pitfalls

  • Fractal dimension is often irrational. Don't expect round numbers.
  • Finite simulations yield approximate D. True fractals require infinite iteration; what you measure is always an estimate with error bars.
  • Two fractals with the same D can look very different. Dimension measures space-filling; it doesn't capture full morphology.
  • In DLA, changing sticking probability changes both the shape and the dimension. Low sticking → denser, higher D. High sticking → spindlier, lower D.

Further reading

  • Witten & Sander, 1981 Physical Review Letters paper — the original DLA model.
