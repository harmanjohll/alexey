Expected Goals (xG)

A probability, not a prediction.

The question. You watch a match. Your team takes 18 shots but scores only once. Were they unlucky, or were the shots actually bad? Gut feel can't tell you — but geometry can.

The rule

xG is a probability between 0 and 1 assigned to every shot, based on where it was taken from.
A shot with xG = 0.3 means: if you took this same shot 100 times, you'd expect to score about 30.
Sum of xG across all shots ≈ expected goals over the long run.

The cheapest xG model uses only two inputs: distance to goal and angle subtended by the goal posts. Wider angle + shorter distance = higher xG. Real professional models layer in shot type (header, free kick, penalty), assist context, and defender pressure — but geometry does most of the work.

Why it matters

Over one match, luck dominates. Over a season (~400 shots), actual goals converge on total xG. A team consistently over-performing its xG is either extraordinary at finishing, or lucky — and the two look identical from a single season of data. Clubs use this to spot under-valued players and over-valued results.

Worked example

A shot from the penalty spot, 11 m out, dead centre.

Angle subtended by the 7.32 m goal = 2 · atan(3.66 / 11) ≈ 37°.
Geometric xG (with a distance-decay factor) ≈ 0.28.

Now move the same shot 5 m wider. Distance barely changes, but the angle collapses to ~18°. xG drops to ~0.10 — roughly a third. That's why "getting closer to goal" matters less than "getting a better angle."

Before you run the simulation

  1. Where on the pitch do you think xG is highest, and why?
  2. Two shots at the same distance — one central, one wide. Same xG, or different? Why?
  3. If a team's total xG across a match is 1.8 but they score 0 goals, what are the two possible reasons — and how would you tell them apart?

Common pitfalls

  • xG tells you about the long run, not the next shot. A 0.9 xG shot still misses 10% of the time.
  • "This team over-performed xG by 15 goals" does not prove their strikers are better. It could be noise. Check next season.
  • Geometric xG can't see defenders, keeper position, pressure, or state of the game. The simple model leaves those out — which is a feature for learning, not a flaw.
  • Sum of xG ≠ guaranteed goals. The Monte Carlo distribution shows the full spread of possible match outcomes.
