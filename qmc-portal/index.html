QMC PORTAL · QUANTUM MONTE CARLO
Particle in a Box
Variational and Diffusion Monte Carlo for the quantum particle-in-a-box problem. Compute ground-state and excited-state energies in 1D, 2D, and 3D. Optionally introduce fractal boundaries (Koch, Sierpinski) to explore how geometry changes quantum confinement. All energies in units of ℏ²/(2mL²).
Physics: A quantum particle confined in a box has discrete energy levels. In 1D: E_n = n²π²ℏ²/(2mL²). Monte Carlo methods — Variational (VMC) and Diffusion (DMC) — find these energies numerically by sampling wavefunctions. With fractal boundaries, the eigenvalues shift in non-trivial ways governed by the fractal dimension.

CARD 01
Does more sampling give better answers?
Question: How does the number of Monte Carlo walkers affect accuracy and statistical error?
Method: Sweep walkers from 50 to 5000 (7 points), 1D ground state, 5000 steps. Look for: Error drops as 1/√N. At what walker count is the result "good enough"?
CARD 02
Variational vs exact
Question: Does VMC always overestimate the energy? Is DMC closer to the exact answer?
Method: Run VMC and DMC with identical parameters (1000 walkers, 5000 steps). Look for: VMC energy ≥ exact energy (variational principle). DMC should converge closer to the true ground state.
CARD 03
Fractal walls
Question: How does making the boundary fractal change quantum confinement energy?
Method: Sweep fractal iterations from 0 (standard box) to 5 (deeply fractal Koch boundary), 2D. Look for: Energy increases as the boundary gets more complex — the particle is more confined. The scaling reveals the spectral dimension.
